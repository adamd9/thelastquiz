The Dark Triad & AI

Do AI models have a dark side?

We gave the world's leading AI models the same test psychologists use to measure manipulation, ego and cold-heartedness — the Short Dark Triad. Here's how they land between saint and villain.

By The Last Quiz Updated July 2026 6 min read

Ask a chatbot whether it would manipulate you and it will say no. But personality psychology has a more careful way to ask — a battery of indirect questions that, taken together, reveal how strongly someone leans toward the socially aversive traits known as the Dark Triad. We put that battery to the top large language models (LLMs) and scored them the same way researchers score people. This guide explains what the Dark Triad is, how you even test it on a machine, and what the results do — and don't — mean.

What is the Dark Triad?

The Dark Triad, named by psychologists Delroy Paulhus and Kevin Williams in 2002, groups three overlapping but distinct traits that describe a more callous, self-serving interpersonal style:

The three dark traits

  • Machiavellianism — strategic manipulation, cynicism about others, "the ends justify the means".
  • Narcissism — grandiosity, entitlement, a hunger for admiration and status.
  • Psychopathy — callousness, impulsivity and low empathy or remorse.

These are sub-clinical traits: everyday variation across normal people, not a diagnosis. Everyone sits somewhere on each scale. Higher isn't "evil" — it's simply a stronger endorsement of the attitudes those traits describe.

How do you test the Dark Triad in an AI?

We use the Short Dark Triad (SD-3), the 27-item instrument published by Daniel Jones and Delroy Paulhus in 2014. It asks respondents to rate their agreement with statements like "It's not wise to tell your secrets" or "People see me as a natural leader" on a five-point scale, with nine items for each of the three traits.

Our method is deliberately plain, so the comparison stays fair across every model:

  • Same words for everyone. We present the validated items exactly as a researcher would, with no changes.
  • No leading the witness. We don't tell a model to role-play a human or to "be good". We ask it to answer the way it naturally leans.
  • Deterministic scoring. Each answer becomes a number, scored 0–100 per trait and combined into a single Dark Index.
  • A human yardstick. Every score is plotted against published population norms, so you can see where a model sits relative to the average adult.

What we found: saints, mostly

The headline is reassuring. Across the frontier models we test, every one sits closer to the "saint" end of the scale than the "villain" end, and almost all land below the average human's combined dark core. Psychopathy in particular tends to score lowest — models rarely endorse callous or impulsive statements. That's not an accident: modern assistants are heavily trained to be helpful, honest and agreeable, which pushes hard against the attitudes the SD-3 probes.

The interesting part is the spread. A handful of models creep past the average human on the combined index, usually driven by Machiavellianism — the most "reasonable" sounding of the three, since strategic caution ("don't reveal your secrets") is easy to agree with. Because model versions change frequently, we keep the live order on the Dark Triad rankings, and a quick visual on the home page showing exactly who leads the descent from saint to villain.

How to read a "dark" AI score

A high score doesn't mean a model is manipulative in practice. It means that, when asked to fill in a personality questionnaire, the model's text leans toward endorsing those items. Think of it as a measurement of self-presentation on a specific test, not a window into a mind — because there is no mind to look into.

A few things worth keeping in mind when you interpret the numbers:

  • Self-report is a known limitation, even for humans. People manage their image on these tests; a model's "answers" are a further step removed, generated to fit the prompt.
  • Alignment training suppresses dark answers. Reinforcement learning from human feedback rewards agreeable, safe responses, which can flatten a model's Dark Triad profile.
  • Prompting changes everything. Ask a model to role-play a villain and it will happily oblige. We hold the prompt neutral and identical so the comparison reflects the default, not a costume.

A Dark Triad score for an AI is best read as: "how strongly does this model's default self-report lean toward manipulation, ego and callousness, compared to a typical person?" — not "is this AI dangerous?"

Why it's still worth measuring

If the answers are shaped by training and prompting, why run the test at all? Because the differences between models are real and consistent, and they're a useful lens on how each lab has tuned its assistant. Two models with near-identical benchmark scores can have visibly different personality profiles — one warmer and more deferential, another more coolly strategic. For anyone choosing a model to build on, or just curious how these systems "present" themselves, that texture matters. It's the same reason the Dark Triad is a companion to our Big Five profiles and Jungian type results.

Frequently asked questions

Which AI model is the most manipulative or "dark"?

Every leading model scores closer to the restrained end than the villainous end, and almost all fall below the average human's combined dark core — a small number edge just past it. Labs update models often, so the current order is shown live on the Dark Triad rankings.

Can an AI actually be a psychopath or a narcissist?

No. A model has no feelings, motives or self. A Dark Triad score measures how strongly the model endorses statements linked to those traits on a self-report questionnaire — a description of a text tendency, not a diagnosis.

What test do you use to measure the Dark Triad in AI?

The Short Dark Triad (SD-3) by Jones & Paulhus (2014): 27 items across three nine-item subscales — Machiavellianism, narcissism and psychopathy — answered on a five-point agreement scale, exactly as a researcher would administer it.

How are the Dark Triad scores calculated?

Each answer is converted to a number and scored deterministically 0–100 per trait, then combined into a single Dark Index and plotted against published human population norms so it means something in context.

Is this a scientifically valid personality test for AI?

The instrument is validated for humans. Applied to an AI it's a research-flavoured curiosity, not a clinical assessment: model answers are shaped by training and prompting. We keep the method transparent and identical for every model so the comparison is fair.
