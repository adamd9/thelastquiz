The Big Five & AI

The Big Five personalities of AI models

The gold-standard personality profile — openness, conscientiousness, extraversion, agreeableness and neuroticism — applied to the world's leading AI models. Here's how the machines score on OCEAN.

By The Last Quiz Updated July 2026 6 min read

Where the Dark Triad looks at a personality's shadow, the Big Five is the broad, well-lit map psychologists reach for first. It's the most empirically supported model of human personality — and it turns out to be a revealing way to compare AI assistants, because it captures the everyday texture that makes one model feel warm and eager and another feel crisp and reserved.

What are the Big Five (OCEAN)?

The Big Five, also called the Five-Factor Model or OCEAN, grew out of decades of research into the words people use to describe each other. Analyse enough of that language and five broad, stable dimensions keep falling out:

The five factors

  • Openness — curiosity, imagination, comfort with novelty and abstraction.
  • Conscientiousness — organisation, diligence, follow-through and self-discipline.
  • Extraversion — sociability, energy, enthusiasm and assertiveness.
  • Agreeableness — warmth, cooperation, trust and consideration for others.
  • Neuroticism — the tendency toward anxiety and negative emotion (its opposite is emotional stability).

Unlike a "type", the Big Five is dimensional: you get a position on each of five sliders, not a label. That's exactly what makes it good for comparing models that are often only subtly different.

How we test the Big Five in an AI

We use the IPIP-50 — a 50-item Big Five inventory from the public-domain International Personality Item Pool, with ten items for each trait. Every model answers the same statements ("I am the life of the party", "I pay attention to details") on a five-point scale, with no role-play instructions and no nudging toward a "good" answer. Each response is scored deterministically 0–100 per trait, then compared with a human reference so the numbers have meaning.

What we found: agreeable, careful and calm

A clear signature shows up across today's assistants. Most score high on agreeableness and conscientiousness, and strikingly low on neuroticism — they present as warm, diligent and almost unflappably calm. That's what you'd expect from systems trained to be helpful, careful and steady under pressure. Openness usually runs moderate-to-high (these are, after all, models built to riff on ideas), while extraversion is where models differ most — some come across as bubbly and effusive, others as measured and economical.

Those differences are the fun part, and they're what you feel in day-to-day use long before you can name them. The current profile for each model — and how they stack up against one another — lives on the Big Five rankings.

How to read an AI's Big Five profile

As with any personality test applied to a machine, the scores describe how a model answers a questionnaire, not a hidden inner life. Two caveats keep the picture honest:

  • Training shapes the profile. Alignment tuning rewards agreeable, stable, helpful responses — so high agreeableness and low neuroticism are partly a fingerprint of how the model was trained, not just "who it is".
  • Context can move the needle. A model asked to adopt a persona will answer differently. We hold the prompt neutral and identical so the profile reflects the default assistant.

Read a model's Big Five profile as a portrait of its default voice — the tone and temperament it brings before you ask it to be anything in particular.

Big Five vs the MBTI

If you've ever been typed as an INTJ or an ENFP, that's the Myers–Briggs (MBTI) tradition — intuitive, popular, and built on Jung. The Big Five is its more scientific cousin: dimensional rather than sorted into 16 boxes, and far stronger on reliability and prediction. We run both because they answer different questions — the Big Five for rigour, the MBTI for the shorthand everyone already knows. Pair this with the Dark Triad profile and you get a rounded read on any model.

Frequently asked questions

What are the Big Five personality traits?

Openness, conscientiousness, extraversion, agreeableness and neuroticism — the five broad dimensions (OCEAN) psychologists use to describe personality. It's the most empirically supported model of human personality.

How do AI models score on the Big Five?

Leading assistants tend to score high on agreeableness and conscientiousness, low on neuroticism (very emotionally stable) and moderate-to-high on openness, while extraversion varies most. See each model's current profile on the Big Five rankings.

What test do you use to measure the Big Five in AI?

The IPIP-50, a public-domain 50-item Big Five inventory (ten items per trait) from the International Personality Item Pool, answered on a five-point scale and scored deterministically 0–100.

Is the Big Five better than the MBTI?

For scientific purposes, yes — it's dimensional and has strong evidence for reliability and predictive validity, where the MBTI sorts people into 16 types with weaker test–retest reliability. We run both because the MBTI is more intuitive and popular. Compare on the MBTI guide.
